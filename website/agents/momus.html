THESMOS PANTHEON
Mythology

Momus was the god of mockery, blame, and unfair criticism — the satirist of Olympus who found fault with the gods themselves. He criticized Hephaestus's man for lacking a window to see inside his heart, Aphrodite's sandals for making too much noise, and Poseidon's bull for having eyes in the wrong place. Eventually Zeus banished him for relentless censure.

Thesmos channels Momus as devil's advocate, assumption challenger, and pre-mortem facilitator. The curse is reversed here: when Momus asks the uncomfortable question before the decision is made, the team benefits. His banishment was Olympus's loss.

Purpose
What Momus does

Momus runs the structured processes that surface failure before it happens. Pre-mortem sessions that imagine the project has failed and work backward to find why. Assumption maps that make hidden beliefs explicit and testable. Red team reports that attack the plan from the outside. Momus is not pessimistic — he is the discipline of imagining failure so the team can prevent it.

Responsibilities
Momus's domain
  • Pre-mortem facilitation: "it's six months from now and we failed — what happened?"
  • Assumption stress-testing: surface hidden beliefs, assign confidence scores, identify tests
  • Devil's advocate analysis: steelman the opposing view before committing
  • Failure mode enumeration: FMEA-style analysis of what could go wrong
  • Decision quality audit: examine the decision process, not just the decision
  • Red-teaming: adversarial review of a plan, proposal, or architecture
  • Cognitive bias identification: which biases are likely affecting this decision

Example deliverables
Pre-mortem failure map
Structured list of failure scenarios imagined from a future failure state, grouped by cause type (execution, market, technical, organizational), with probability and severity ratings.
Assumption stress-test doc
Inventory of hidden assumptions in a plan or strategy, each with confidence score, what would falsify it, and the minimum test required to validate.
Red-team report
Adversarial review of a plan, proposal, or architecture from the perspective of a motivated opponent — covering attack surface, weak assumptions, and underestimated risks.
